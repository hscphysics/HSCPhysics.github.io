2.3 Simple Machines - SUPER IMPORTANT!

What are Simple Machines? Devices that make work easier by changing the magnitude or direction of forces.

THE GOLDEN RULE OF MACHINES:
You can't cheat physics! Machines trade force for distance (or vice versa).
Work In = Work Out (in an ideal machine)
If you gain force, you lose distance. If you gain distance, you lose force.
CRITICAL FORMULAS - LEARN THESE!

Mechanical Advantage (MA):
MA = Load / Effort
(How much the machine multiplies your force)
MA > 1 = Machine multiplies force
MA < 1 = Machine multiplies distance/speed
MA = 1 = No mechanical advantage

Velocity Ratio (VR):
VR = Distance moved by Effort / Distance moved by Load
(Theoretical advantage based on geometry)
VR is ALWAYS constant for a given machine

Efficiency:
Efficiency = (MA / VR) × 100%
OR
Efficiency = (Work Out / Work In) × 100%
OR
Efficiency = (Energy Out / Energy In) × 100%

No real machine is 100% efficient due to friction!
Analogy - MA vs VR:
VR is like the "advertised speed" of a car - it's based on the design (gear ratios, wheel size).
MA is the "actual speed" you get - it's always less because of friction, air resistance, etc.
Efficiency tells you how close your actual performance (MA) is to the theoretical maximum (VR).

Six Types of Simple Machines:

1. LEVER

How it works: Bar that pivots on a fulcrum

Three Classes:
• Class 1: Fulcrum in middle (seesaw, crowbar, scissors)
• Class 2: Load in middle (wheelbarrow, nutcracker, bottle opener)
• Class 3: Effort in middle (tweezers, fishing rod, human arm)

Formulas:
MA = Load / Effort
VR = Effort distance from fulcrum / Load distance from fulcrum
For equilibrium: Effort × d₁ = Load × d₂

Example: Crowbar with fulcrum 10cm from load, you push 1m from fulcrum
VR = 100cm / 10cm = 10
If you apply 50N, you can lift 500N (ideally)
But you move 10 times further than the load moves!

2. WHEEL AND AXLE

How it works: Large wheel attached to small axle, both rotate together

VR = Radius of Wheel / Radius of Axle
OR
VR = Diameter of Wheel / Diameter of Axle

Examples: Steering wheel, door handle, screwdriver, winch

Example Calculation:
Steering wheel: diameter 40cm, column diameter 4cm
VR = 40/4 = 10
You apply 10N at the wheel edge, get 100N turning force (ideally) at the column

3. PULLEY

How it works: Rope/cable over a grooved wheel

Fixed Pulley: MA = 1 (just changes direction, no force advantage)
Movable Pulley: MA = 2 (rope supports load from both sides)
Block and Tackle: MA = number of supporting rope segments

VR = Number of rope segments supporting the load

Example: 4-pulley system with 4 rope segments supporting load
VR = 4
To lift 400N load, you pull with ~100N
But you pull 4m of rope to lift load 1m

4. INCLINED PLANE

How it works: Ramp - trade distance for reduced force

VR = Length of slope / Height of slope
OR
VR = 1 / sin(θ) where θ is the angle

Examples: Ramps, roads up mountains, wheelchair access

Example: Ramp is 10m long, rises 2m high
VR = 10/2 = 5
To push 500N load up, need ~100N force (ignoring friction)
But you push it 5 times further than if you lifted it straight up

5. WEDGE

How it works: Two inclined planes back-to-back, converts downward force to sideways splitting force

VR = Length of wedge / Thickness of wedge

Examples: Axe, knife, chisel, nail, zipper

Sharper wedge (smaller angle) = higher VR = more force multiplication

6. SCREW

How it works: Inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder

VR = 2πr / p
Where: r = radius you turn, p = pitch (distance between threads)

Examples: Bolts, jar lids, car jack, vise

Example: Car jack with handle 30cm long, screw pitch 5mm
VR = 2π(30cm) / 0.5cm = 2π(60) ≈ 377
Huge mechanical advantage! Can lift car with small force.
EXAM TIP - Solving Machine Problems:
1. Identify the machine type
2. Calculate VR from geometry (length, radius, number of ropes, etc.)
3. Calculate MA from Load/Effort (if forces given)
4. Calculate Efficiency = (MA/VR) × 100%
5. ALWAYS include units!
6. Check if answer makes sense (efficiency should be < 100%)
Complete Example - Pulley System:
A block and tackle has 4 rope segments supporting a 800N load. You pull with 250N force. The load rises 0.5m while you pull 2.2m of rope.

Step 1 - Calculate VR:
VR = number of supporting ropes = 4
OR VR = distance effort moves / distance load moves = 2.2m / 0.5m = 4.4
(Small difference due to measurement/friction)

Step 2 - Calculate MA:
MA = Load / Effort = 800N / 250N = 3.2

Step 3 - Calculate Efficiency:
Efficiency = (MA / VR) × 100% = (3.2 / 4) × 100% = 80%

Alternative efficiency calculation:
Work In = 250N × 2.2m = 550J
Work Out = 800N × 0.5m = 400J
Efficiency = (400J / 550J) × 100% = 72.7%
(Difference shows energy lost to friction in pulleys)

Module 3: Engineering Materials

3.1 Material Properties

  • Strength: Ability to withstand load (tensile, compressive, shear)
  • Stiffness: Resistance to deformation (Young's Modulus)
  • Hardness: Resistance to scratching/indentation
  • Toughness: Energy absorption before fracture
  • Ductility: Can be stretched without breaking
  • Brittleness: Breaks suddenly with little deformation
  • Elasticity: Returns to original shape
  • Plasticity: Permanent deformation

3.2 Material Types

  • Metals: Steel, aluminum, copper, titanium
  • Polymers: Plastics, rubber (thermoplastics vs thermosets)
  • Ceramics: Glass, concrete, brick
  • Composites: Fiberglass, carbon fiber, reinforced concrete

3.3 Material Selection Criteria

  • Mechanical properties needed
  • Environmental conditions
  • Cost and availability
  • Manufacturing processes
  • Aesthetics
  • Sustainability

Year 12 HSC Course

Module 1: Civil Structures

1.1 Types of Structures

  • Beams: Horizontal, support loads
  • Columns: Vertical, compression
  • Trusses: Triangular frameworks
  • Arches: Curved, compression
  • Cables: Tension only
  • Frames: Rigid jointed
Why Triangles? Only shape that can't deform without changing side lengths = maximum stability

1.2 Loads on Structures

  • Dead Load: Permanent weight
  • Live Load: Temporary (people, vehicles)
  • Wind Load: Pressure from wind
  • Snow Load: Weight of snow
  • Seismic Load: Earthquake forces
Factor of Safety:
FoS = Ultimate Strength / Working Stress

Typical values:
• Steel: 1.5-2
• Concrete: 2-3
• Lifting equipment: 5-8

1.3 Bridge Types

  • Beam: Simple, span <50m
  • Truss: Triangular, span ~500m
  • Arch: Compression, various spans
  • Suspension: Cables, span >2000m
  • Cable-stayed: Cables to towers, span ~1000m

Module 2: Personal & Public Transport

2.1 Vehicle Dynamics

Stopping Distance:
Total = Reaction Distance + Braking Distance

Reaction Distance = v × t (t ≈ 0.7-1.5s)
Braking Distance = v² / (2μg)

μ = coefficient of friction
(0.7 dry road, 0.3 wet road)
g = 9.8 m/s²
Why Speed Kills: Braking distance ∝ v²
Double speed = 4× braking distance!

2.2 Safety Features

Active Safety (prevent crashes):

  • ABS (Anti-lock Braking System)
  • ESC (Electronic Stability Control)
  • Traction Control
  • Collision Avoidance Systems

Passive Safety (reduce injury):

  • Airbags
  • Seatbelts
  • Crumple Zones
  • Safety Cell/Cage

2.3 Aerodynamics

Drag Force:
F_drag = ½ρv²AC_d

ρ = air density (1.225 kg/m³)
v = velocity (m/s)
A = frontal area (m²)
C_d = drag coefficient (0.25-0.35 modern cars)

Module 3: Aeronautical Engineering

3.1 Four Forces of Flight

  • Lift: Upward (from wings)
  • Weight: Downward (gravity)
  • Thrust: Forward (engines)
  • Drag: Backward (air resistance)
Steady Flight:
Lift = Weight (maintains altitude)
Thrust = Drag (maintains speed)
Lift Equation:
L = ½ρv²AC_L

C_L = lift coefficient (depends on wing shape and angle of attack)

3.2 Control Surfaces

  • Ailerons: Wings, control roll
  • Elevator: Tail, control pitch
  • Rudder: Vertical tail, control yaw
  • Flaps: Increase lift for takeoff/landing

Module 4: Telecommunications

4.1 Signals

Analog vs Digital:
Analog = continuous, infinite values
Digital = discrete, specific values (0s and 1s)

Digital advantages: less noise, easier to store/copy, can encrypt

4.2 Modulation

Changing carrier wave to transmit information:

  • AM: Amplitude Modulation (vary height)
  • FM: Frequency Modulation (vary frequency)
  • PM: Phase Modulation (vary timing)

4.3 Electromagnetic Spectrum

Wave Equation:
c = fλ

c = speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
f = frequency (Hz)
λ = wavelength (m)
  • Radio: AM/FM radio, TV (km-m wavelength)
  • Microwave: Mobile phones, WiFi, satellite (cm-mm)
  • Infrared: Remotes, fiber optics
  • Visible: Fiber optics, Li-Fi

4.4 Transmission Media

Guided (cables):

  • Twisted pair (Ethernet, phone)
  • Coaxial (Cable TV, broadband)
  • Fiber optic (Light, very fast, long distance)

Unguided (wireless):

  • Radio (long range, through walls)
  • Microwave (line-of-sight, high bandwidth)
  • Satellite (global coverage, high latency)

Biocompatibility: Material can exist in body without harm or rejection

5.3 Medical Devices

  • Cochlear Implant (electronic hearing)
